Short answer: one combined ledger with the account-type filter you already have is right for browsing — but for spending analytics, never merge them naively, because credit-card payments from checking would double-count spending.

Here's the reasoning, grounded in what the app currently does:

Current state. The dashboard already does "separate-ish": index.html has Credit Cards / Checking & Savings tabs that pass account_type to the API, and the charts are hardcoded to account_type=credit_card (app.js:116, 153).

Why the two types resist merging. The semantics of a row differ by account type:

- On a credit card, nearly every transaction is spending. Summing debits gives a meaningful "what did I spend" number.
- In a checking account, rows are a mix of income (salary), true spending (rent, utilities), transfers between your own accounts, and — critically — credit card bill payments. That $800 payment to Capital One is the same money as the $800 of individual charges already sitting on the card side. Merge the two streams into one spending chart and every dollar spent on a card is counted twice.

What works best in practice (this is the pattern Mint/YNAB/Monarch converged on):

1. Transactions table: together by default, filterable. A single ledger answers "where did I buy X" without the user remembering which account it hit. Your tabs already act as a filter over one table, which is effectively this — you could add an "All" tab cheaply. An account/bank column in the table matters more than the split itself.
2. Spending charts: unified, but transfer-aware. The honest fix isn't "cards only" (which is what you have now — it silently hides cash spending like rent paid from checking) and isn't "everything" (double counting). It's: include both types, but tag CC payments and inter-account transfers as Transfer and exclude that category from spending summaries. You already have a Transfers concept in the frontend (per the recent commit history), so the seam exists.
3. Balances: always separate. Bank balance is an asset, card balance is a liability — summing them is meaningless except as a net-worth line. The accounts page is the right home for that.

So: keep the tabbed table (maybe add "All"), and the real design work is transfer detection/exclusion in the summaries — that's the thing standing between you and a truthful combined spending vment that if you want.
